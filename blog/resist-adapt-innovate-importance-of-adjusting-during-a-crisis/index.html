Resist, adapt, innovate – importance of adjusting during a crisis

Author Dorian Derežić
Category Company
Date Mar 30, 2020
7 min read

Crisis doesn’t care. It doesn’t care about the work you do, your processes, your projects or your company’s financial stability. It has its own path and impacts anything that stands in its way. So when it comes to successful crisis management, it’s up to you to adjust and find a way to counter it!

In our previous blog about building a crisis-proof business, we mentioned the importance of changing your mindset when it comes to leading a company and the way you approach things when a crisis strikes. We talked about how crucial it is to implement changes that are long-lasting – ones that will benefit your company indefinitely, and not just for the duration of the crisis. In this blog, we’ll go a little deeper into what adjusting to a crisis really means when it comes to changing your business processes and projects.

That’s how we always used to do things around here

Throw that mindset into the trash. I’m serious – when it’s all good and without a crisis in sight, that type of mindset is usually frowned upon (especially in rapidly changing industries like the IT), because it’s all about keeping the status quo and doing just enough to keep your company stable. When a crisis strikes, every industry turns into a rapidly changing industry, because that’s the way you minimize the impact. And if your standard way of operating is ‘but that’s how we always used to do things around here’, you probably won’t have that battle-hardened experience needed to make quick decisions, implement changes and stay ahead of the crisis. And that type of experience is always great to have, regardless of the situation!

So my advice to you is – throw the ‘that’s how we always used to do things around here’ mindset into the trash and use this current crisis to make your company more crisis-proof. Yeah, it might feel a little rusty at first, but long-term benefits of operating in a way that prioritizes change are tenfold.

Let me give you an example of this that just happened. Croatian bureaucracy, probably like the majority of bureaucracies around the world, is all about strict procedures, paperwork, having everything neatly signed and stamped and delivered to the right office and then going to the next office to get another form you need to fill out and then return to the previous office just to find out you forgot to bring a crucial document and… you get the point. And if you thought about emailing those documents, well that was a no. Before this current crisis, that was the standard way of operating… fast-forward into the crisis and now it’s email all the things – and it works much smoother, faster, it’s more streamlined and the overall user experience is miles ahead of what it used to be.

Yeah, my point here is – if Croatian bureaucracy can change the way it operates in a time of crisis and let go of its old ways, I believe your company can too!

Think fast

Or better yet, think about your company being fast, even faster and constantly adjusting your internal processes and ways of doing things to be two steps ahead of the crisis. Here’s the deal, times of crises are times of uncertainty, so you no longer have years to test and implement a new tool, build a new in-house team or switch your sales processes from being primarily offline to being online-first (like a lot of brick and mortar stores are now doing by launching webshops to keep their businesses active). At best, you have a few months to adjust, so make sure to utilize that time efficiently – use tools to gather data about your business and really dig into it, analyze the data you have, locate your strengths and painpoints and start working on those that might yield you the most benefits in the shortest amount of time (if you want to know more about gathering and analyzing data, and how your company can benefit from it, get in touch).

That is something we did at Bornfight when this entire situation started and we rapidly needed to adjust to new ways our clients started doing business. As their situations changed, they needed more rapid improvements to software and solutions we created for them – so we analyzed our processes and saw that in order to meet their new needs, we had to speed up our own process of onboarding new people onto projects We used to have a much more complex onboarding system, but this change prompted us to reanalyze everything, take out the steps that have proven to be inefficient or unneeded and create a new way of operating. And now our process of onboarding people onto projects is much faster and more streamlined than before – I’d even say it’s as close to plug-and-play as we can be at this moment.

To conclude this section, I’ll say this – time is the only commodity you can’t get back or create more of it, and it’s even more valuable in a time of crisis when everything is on a deadline, so be fast and maximize the time that’s available to you.

Think flexible

Another aspect of adjusting your business processes or projects to an ongoing crisis is to focus on being as flexible as possible. Let me paint you a picture – when push comes to shove, flexible businesses survive by adapting, going around a crisis and dodging its impacts, while businesses that aren’t flexible survive only if they’re strong enough to sustain the full impact of a crisis. This aspect of business flexibility is closely connected to the section about distancing yourself and your company from the ‘that’s how we always used to do things here’ mindset, so you can think of it as an extension. Here’s what I mean by it – you can’t be flexible if you want to do your thing the old way, but you can throw away that ‘old way’ mindset without being flexible. Ideally, you want to do both, and when it comes to a crisis, you’ll kind of need to.

Again, we’ll go back to gathering and analyzing the available data about your business, as data is your key decision-making guide in a time of crisis. Write down all of your processes with a short description of how your business is currently doing it, and then see what can be upgraded in a way that counters the crisis.

Here’s an example:

  • Sales process – does it have to be offline or can you do it through a webshop?
  • Worker training – can 1-on1 training be substituted for online workshops?
  • Order management – are you still doing it by hand or can you automate with software?
  • Productivity tracking – do you need punch cards or is a time tracking tool better?

There’s always a way to upgrade a process – the key is just to analyze your business. Analyze why, how and what you’re currently doing, and then create a plan of how it should be done in order to better fit your company now, the current (crisis) situation and the way you want your company to grow in the future.

Let me give you another example based on a process we just changed at Bornfight – discovery workshops. Product discovery workshops are a key element of every project we work on – before we start with any kind of design or development, we fly our clients to our office or visit theirs, and then conduct a very extensive 4 day workshop during which we dig deep into our client’s business, uncover their strengths and painpoints, and create a detailed production plan for a solution they hired us to build. From the day we implemented discovery workshops into our process, we always conducted them face-to-face. Now that borders are being closed and a lot of people are in self-isolation, having workshops face to face is no longer an option – but does that mean we can no longer take on any new projects? Of course it doesn’t. What we needed to do was take the core principles of discovery workshops, adjust them a bit until we could transfer them online and now we have virtual discovery workshops (a blog with tips on how to implement them is coming soon, so stay tuned) – technology was already there and all we needed was a bit of flexibility when it came to changing our mindset and approach.

Was that change a piece of cake? No. But are its benefits worth it? Absolutely.

Think different

Not in terms of Apple, but in terms of how you can use various tools, platforms, processes, projects or any other resource or asset in a way it’s not yet been used – this is where your creativity and lateral thinking needs to kick in.

Here’s a great example. You’ve heard about TikTok? It’s an extremely popular mobile application with more than 500 million active users that enables them to record and post videos of themselves that are only a few seconds long. TikTok was never intended to be a platform for streaming long-form content or movies. Well, that was the case until this crisis started and Huanxi Media Group, a Chinese media company, saw they’ll lose quite a bit of money because movie theaters are closed and they can’t launch their new movie. They got in contact with the owners of TikTok, presented the idea to stream the movie on the platform, and now you can watch a 2 hour movie on TikTok – a platform primarily intended for 60-second videos.

Another awesome example of thinking differently in a time of crisis comes from a Chinese e-commerce giant, JD Logistics. To counter the impact of the crisis on their business and to help deliver medical care, JD Logistics utilized their fleet of self-driving vehicles to deliver supplies across a heavily quarantined Wuhan. And in addition to utilizing delivery vehicles, JD Logistics also provided access to its Level-4 autonomous driving solutions, thus enabling even more autonomous delivery robot manufacturers to update their technologies in the wake of this crisis, which is bound to result in an even faster advancement of self-driving technologies and solutions.

The point here is clear – take a look at your assets and resources, or those of your clients and partners. There’s bound to be at least one that can be used in a different, creative way!
